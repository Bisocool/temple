The Lord of the Chariot

Parthasarathi: the friend who took the reins.

On the field of Kurukshetra, when the warrior Arjuna let his bow fall in despair, Krishna did not leave his side. As Parthasarathi, the charioteer of Partha, He turned a moment of collapse into the most luminous teaching the world has known. The Bhagavad Gita is that conversation, eighteen chapters of a friend speaking a soul back to its courage.

Our temple is built around that teaching, not a single shrine alone but the living wisdom it carries. We call it jnana, the knowledge that frees. Here the chariot, the war within, and the quiet voice of guidance are remembered every day, in song, in lamp-light, and in the verses you carry home.

Whether you arrive in person or through this screen, you are received the same way: as a devotee come for darshan, the grace of beholding and being beheld by the Lord.

The night the Lord was born.

Janmashtami marks the midnight birth of Sri Krishna in the prison of Mathura. We keep vigil through the evening with bhajan and abhishekam, the bathing of the deity, until the sacred hour arrives and the cradle is rocked. Prasadam is offered to all who stay.

Temple Etiquette
